Breadboard


How to Use a Breadboard

A breadboard helps you in connecting electronic components together, as illustrated in the picture on the right. It is very common when building prototypes.

The picture below on the left shows a close-up of the breadboard. The idea is that the holes in the board can be used to plug in the components (resistors, LEDs, wires, etc.). Internally, the board provides connections between certain rows or columns of holes. This essentially means that these holes behave as if they are connected together by wires. The picture below on the right shows these internal connections. You can see, for example, that the column of holes labeled '+' are all connected together. The same is true for columns labeled '-' (more on why they are labeled this way later). The holes in the center of the breadboard are connected together not in columns, but in rows. For example, in row 1, the holes in columns a, b, c, d, e are all connected.
You will use the breadboard to build the electronic circuits we describe in the tutorials. In these tutorials, we will show a schematic, which is a symbolic way of showing what needs to be connected to what. You then have to figure out how this translates into actual connections made with wires and the breadboard. For example, on the right, we have shown the schematic similar to the one in the LED Blink Tutorial. The squared triangles signify the pins on the Arduino. You can see that we need to connect the GND pin (GND stands for 'ground', which also corresponds to 0V) of the Arduino, to one side of the LED. The other side of the LED needs to be connected to one side of a resistor (of value 220Ω, see the explanation on resistors). The other side of the resistor in turn needs to be connected to pin 13 on the Arduino. The diagram below illustrates how this translates to actual wires on a breadboard. The colored lines signify jumper wires. Trace the connections in this diagram to verify this indeed corresponds to the schematic on the right. In the tutorials, only the schematics will be given and it is up to you to translate them into the corresponding physical connections of wires and breadboard.




Finally, a quick word on "good wiring". As noted before, there are two columns of labeled '+' on either side of the breadboard. The goal of these holes is to provide plenty of places that give you access to 5V (also called the 'supply voltage') as this value is often needed in our circuits. To accomplish this, you need to only connect one of the holes with a jumper wire to the 5V of the Arduino, and, because of the internal connections within the breadboard, all the other '+' holes now provide 5V as well. Note that the '+' columns on both sides of the board are not connected internally and you would need to connect both sides separately with a wire. Similarly, you can connect the column labeled '-' to GND of the Arduino to create many more places where you can get GND (0V). The wiring example above illustrates this use of the '+' and '-' holes, which are also called the 'power rails'.
